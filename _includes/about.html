About

I am currently a postdoc at Casey Dunn's lab in the Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Yale University.

I am broadly interested in using genomic tools to understand the evolution of marine invertebrates, especially the deep-sea animals. My research falls into several domains.


  • Siboglinidae

    Tubeworms are gutless and solely depend on endosymbionts for nutrition and found in deep-sea chemosynthetic communities, such as vents, seeps, whale bones or wood falls. We study the evolution, host-symbiont association and adaptation to extreme environments to understand their unique symbiosis relationship.

  • Animal phylogenetics

    Phylogenetics is the study of evolutionary relationships. We are particularly interested in deep animal phylogeny, especially the relationship of early diverging lineages such as ctenophores, sponges, cnidarians and placozoans.

  • HIV Phlodynamics

    Phylodynamics is the study of how epidemiological, immunological, and evolutionary processes act and potentially interact to shape viral phylogenies. We are currently developing new methods and models for phylodynamic of HIV evolution as well as explore conventional methods on Real data

  • Comparative and functional genomics

    I am also broadly interested in animal and symbionts genome evolution, especially the deep-sea organisms and early diverging animals.

  • Shipworms

    Shipworms are wood-boring bivalves and can live in wood falls both shallow water and deep sea. We are particular interested in deep-sea shipworms Xylophagaidae, including phylogeny, population connectivity and host-symbiont associations.
